I am Keaton C. Green, a current Shriver Peaceworker Fellow and a graduate student in the Department of Geography and Environmental Systems at UMBC, focusing on critical urban geography. My work integrates community practice, advanced spatial analysis, and critical approaches to urban environments. 

Before graduate school, I served as a Peace Corps volunteer in Benin, West Africa, working as a food security specialist focused on access, revenue generation, and nutrition. My efforts supported community-level resilience and aligned development that links household well-being to resource systems and local capacity building.

Currently employed at Healthy Neighborhoods Inc., I support homeowner repair programs, senior stabilization initiatives, reinvestment strategies, and the everyday infrastructures that contribute to neighborhood stability. This work resonates with urban political economy frameworks that examine how community well-being emerges through policy, investment, and unequal spatial arrangements. My specific tasks are: program management of the Senior Legacy Homeowner Repair Grants using state funds, data management and refinement, and assistance with the Green Capital and Loan programs. Programs seen at [Healthy  Neighborhoods Inc.](https://healthyneighborhoods.org/)

I am a technically skilled GIS analyst who designs and executes complex multi-stage workflows in ArcGIS Pro, QGIS, and R, often involving in-depth spatial analysis, geodatabase management, tract-level modeling, and multi-scalar data integration. Certified in 2022, with a focus on cartography and critical GIS. I will also receive a master's certificate in 2027, highlighting my critical GIS skills and a more solidified application of remote sensing. 

My current projects span Baltimore's neighborhoods, using the city's real property and demographic data, as well as education and access data. My most recent projects have highlighted developmental initiatives around vacancies. I have also examined New York City's 311 patterns, COVID-19 ZIP-to-tract apportionment, and Baltimore DPW data reflected through a lens of contemporary critical geographic methods that connect urban problems to spatial data and analytic rigor.

My research focuses on cultural capital, vacancy, reinvestment patterns, boundary making, and the governance of place. I draw on authors such as Stewart, Baird, Colburn, Haraway, and Carter to analyze how inequality becomes embedded in landscapes through structural forces, symbolic meanings, and everyday spatial practices. My reasoning is that this reflects the interpretive and critical traditions in human geography, space, culture, and power are understood as co-producing urban life.

My background in Peace Corps food security work and my current applied urban research illustrate and give understanding to how geographic practice connects global development perspectives with local spatial dynamics.
