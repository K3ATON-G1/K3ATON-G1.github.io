Keaton C. Green a current Shriver Peaceworker Fellow and a graduate student in the Geography and Environmental Systems department at UMBC, where his work integrates community practice, advanced spatial analysis, and critical approaches to urban environments. 

Before graduate school, he served as a Peace Corps volunteer, working as a food security specialist focused on access, revenue generation, and nutrition. His efforts supported community-level resilience and aligned development that links household well-being to resource systems and local capacity building.

With Healthy Neighborhoods Inc. Keaton supports homeowner repair programs, senior stabilization initiatives, reinvestment strategies, and the everyday infrastructures that contribute to neighborhood stability. This work resonates with urban political economy frameworks that examine how community well-being emerges through policy, investment, and unequal spatial arrangements. Keaton's specific tasks are: program management of the Senior Legacy Homeowner Repair Grants using state funds, data management and refinement, and assistance with the Green Capital and Loan programs. Programs seen at https://healthyneighborhoods.org/.

Keaton is a technically skilled GIS analyst who designs and executes complex multi-stage workflows in ArcGIS Pro, Q-GIS, and R, often involving spatial joins, geodatabase management, tract-level modeling, and multi-scalar data integration. Certified in 2022, with a focus on cartography and critical GIS. He will also receive a master's certificate in 2027, highlighting his critical GIS skills and a more solidified application of remote sensing. 

His current projects span Baltimore's neighborhoods, working with the city's real property data, and high-level examinations of housing data. These projects often highlight developmental initiatives around vacancies. He has also examined New York City rat complaint patterns, COVID 19 ZIP-to-tract apportionment, and other comparative urban studies, reflecting contemporary/critical geographic methods that connect urban problems to spatial data and analytic rigor.

Keaton’s research focuses on cultural capital, vacancy, reinvestment patterns, boundary making, and the governance of place. He draws on authors such as Stewart, Baird, Colburn, Haraway, and Carter to analyze how inequality becomes embedded in landscapes through structural forces, symbolic meanings, and everyday spatial practices. This reflects interpretive and critical traditions in human geography, where space, culture, and power are understood as co-producing urban life.
